From Classical Computing to Quantum Computing: classical computers process information using bits, where each bit can only exist in one of two states: 0 or 1. Complex computations are built by combining logical operations into increasingly sophisticated circuits. Quantum computing extends this framework by replacing classical bits with qubits, which are described mathematically as vectors in a complex Hilbert space. Unlike classical bits, qubits can exist in superpositions of the states 0 and 1, allowing quantum computers to represent many computational possibilities simultaneously. 

The Mathematics Behind Quantum Information: Quantum computing is fundamentally built upon mathematics. Throughout the MathQuantum lectures, we explored how complex numbers, linear algebra, and Hilbert spaces provide the mathematical language for describing quantum systems. Quantum states are represented as vectors, while quantum operations correspond to unitary matrices that transform these vectors without losing information. This mathematical framework makes it possible to analyze quantum systems using familiar tools such as matrices, vector spaces, and linear transformations, while extending them into the complex domain.

Quantum Phenomena: Two concepts distinguish quantum computation from classical computation. The first is superposition, which allows a qubit to exist as a combination of basis states until measurement. The second is entanglement, in which multiple qubits become correlated in ways that cannot be explained by classical physics alone. These properties enable quantum algorithms to manipulate information differently from classical algorithms and form the basis for many proposed quantum speedups and communication protocols.

Quantum Algorithms: Quantum information is processed through quantum gates, which are represented mathematically by unitary matrices acting on qubit states. By combining these gates into quantum circuits,researchers have developed algorithms that can outperform classical approaches for certain computational problems. One example introduced during the program was the Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm, which demonstrates how quantum computation can reduce the number of queries needed to solve a particular problem compared to any deterministic classical algorithm. Although not every problem benefits from quantum computation, these examples illustrate that quantum mechanics can fundamentally change computatinoal complexity for specific tasks. 

Connection to Voting Systems: Modern elections involve a variety of mathematically challenging computational problems, such as efficient analysis of voter preferences. Many of these problems become increasingly difficult as election size and complexity grow. This project investigates whether concepts from QIS could eventually provide new approaches to addressing these challenges. Rather than proposing an entirely new voting system, the goal is to explore the math connections between quantum computing and voting theory and evaluate where quantum methods may offer meaningful advantages.
