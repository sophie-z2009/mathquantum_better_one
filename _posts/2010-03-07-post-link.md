From Classical Computing to Quantum Computing: classical computers process information using bits, where each bit can only exist in one of two states: 0 or 1. Complex computations are built by combining logical operations into increasingly sophisticated circuits. Quantum computing extends this framework by replacing classical bits with qubits, which are described mathematically as vectors in a compled Hilbert space. Unlike classical bits, qubits can exist in superpositions of the states 0 and 1, allowing quantum computers to represent many computational possibilities simultaneously. 

The Mathematics Behind Quantum Information: Quantum computing is fundamentally built upon mathematics. Throughout the MathQuantum lectures, we explored how complex numbers, linear algebra, and Hilbert spaces provide the mathematical language for describing quantum systems. Quantum states are represented as vectors, while quantum operations correspond to unitary matrices that transform these vectors without losing information. This mathematical framework makes it possible to analyze quantum systems using familiar tools such as matrices, vector spaces, and linear transformations, while extending them into the complex domain
